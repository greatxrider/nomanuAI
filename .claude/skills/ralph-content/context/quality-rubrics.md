# Quality Rubrics

Quality evaluation criteria for all content types. Use these to self-critique and decide when content is ready.

---

## Newsletter Quality Rubric (Every.to 8-Point)

Evaluate each criterion. Content is ready when all 8 pass.

### 1. Specificity Check
**Question:** Does EVERY significant claim include names, numbers, and timeframes?

**Passes when:**
- Names: Specific people, companies, products (never "experts say" or "a startup")
- Numbers: Exact figures ("$2.5B" not "billions")
- Timeframes: Specific periods ("between early 2025 and fall" not "recently")
- The piece passes the "Ctrl+F test" — searching for "many," "several," "significant," "experts" returns zero vague results

**Fails when:** More than 2 paragraphs lack specific names/numbers

### 2. Evidence Cascade Present
**Question:** Are there 3+ specific examples stacked together for major claims?

**Passes when:**
- Pattern appears: "[Company A] achieved [metric]. Similarly, [Company B] [result]. As did [Company C] with [data]."
- Uses connectors: "similarly," "as did," "including"
- At least one evidence cascade appears for a key claim

**Fails when:** Major claims rely on single examples or generalizations

### 3. Colon Technique Used
**Question:** Does the piece use colon technique to introduce key concepts?

**Passes when:**
- Patterns like "Here's what matters: [insight]" or "The key limitation: [issue]" appear
- Used 3+ times for thesis introduction, framework points, or clarifications
- Strategic use (not just in lists)

**Fails when:** No strategic colon usage or fewer than 3 instances

### 4. Clean Punctuation
**Question:** Is the piece free of em-dashes, using periods, colons, commas, and parentheses instead?

**Allowed alternatives:**
1. Clarification: "The inflammasome (NLRP3 specifically) acts as..."
2. Context/aside: "The writer, now at Pace Capital, explored..."
3. Contrast: "Not the best models. The ones that own infrastructure."

**Passes when:** Zero em-dashes appear. Periods, colons, commas, and parentheses handle all clarification, aside, and contrast functions.

**Fails when:** Any em-dash (—) appears in the content

### 5. One-Sentence Emphasis
**Question:** Are there strategic one-sentence paragraphs at key moments?

**Passes when:**
- 3-6 one-sentence paragraphs appear
- Placed after building context, delivering punchlines
- Examples: "It's pure ruthlessness." "And it's not close." "The timing matters more than the dose."

**Fails when:** No one-sentence paragraphs, or more than 6 (overused), or multiple in a row

### 6. Concrete Over Abstract
**Question:** Does the opening ground readers in something tangible?

**Hook types that pass:**
- Specific case: "Dr. Sarah Chen at Stanford saw..."
- Paradox: "The standard approach assumes... But..."
- Metaphor with sensory detail

**Fails when:**
- Opening with "In today's rapidly evolving..." or "Longevity medicine is..."
- First paragraph lacks concrete details (names, places, numbers, sensory info)

### 7. Voice Authenticity
**Question:** Does it match Every.to voice principles?

**Passes when:**
- Intellectually rigorous yet accessible
- Confident assertions backed by evidence
- Personal without self-indulgence
- Uses characteristic phrases: "Just to be real with you," "Here's what that means"
- No hedging with "might" when "will" is meant

**Fails when:** Feels generic, corporate, or excessively hedged

### 8. Forward-Looking Conclusion
**Question:** Does the conclusion point forward, not summarize?

**Patterns that pass:**
- "And the next [timeframe] will determine [stakes]"
- "If you [condition], you need to [action]"

**Fails when:**
- "In conclusion, we've covered..." or "To summarize the key points..."
- Ending discusses what was said instead of implications

## LinkedIn Quality Rubric (14-Point)

For LinkedIn posts derived from newsletters or standalone.

### Hook Criteria (3 points)
1. **pattern_interrupt**: Does the first line create a pattern interrupt? TRUE if it stops the scroll with something unexpected.
2. **hook_under_150_chars**: Is the hook under 150 characters? TRUE if the opening line is concise and punchy.
3. **creates_curiosity**: Does the hook create genuine curiosity while signaling the topic? TRUE if the reader knows what the post is about AND wants to keep scrolling. Fails if the hook is too vague ("told me something I can't stop thinking about") or gives away the full insight upfront. See "Curiosity Architecture" in voice patterns.

### Structure Criteria (4 points)
4. **has_clear_thesis**: Is there a clear, specific thesis? TRUE if the main argument is obvious within first 3 lines.
5. **uses_line_breaks**: Are line breaks used effectively? TRUE if single sentences or short phrases have their own lines.
6. **avoids_wall_of_text**: No paragraphs longer than 3 sentences? TRUE if all paragraphs are digestible.
7. **follows_hook_expand_close**: Does it follow Hook → Expand → Close structure?

### Content Criteria (4 points)
8. **has_specific_numbers**: Does it include specific numbers or data points? TRUE if at least one concrete number appears.
9. **avoids_jargon**: Is it free of unexplained jargon? TRUE if all terms are accessible.
10. **has_original_insight**: Is there a non-obvious insight? TRUE if it says something readers haven't heard before. For tool/tech evaluations: security stats and vulnerability counts are the *obvious* angle. Practitioner experience (bugginess, daily friction, operational burden, who's hyping it and why) is where original insight lives. See "Technology Evaluation Lens" in voice patterns.
11. **stays_focused**: Does it focus on one idea? TRUE if not trying to cover multiple topics.

### Engagement Criteria (3 points)
12. **has_subtle_cta**: Is there a call-to-action that feels like a narrative resolution, not a bolted-on promotion? TRUE if the CTA resolves the curiosity gap the post built (e.g., "I asked him to walk through the full system. He's joining us Friday."). Fails if it reads like an ad ("Register for our webinar! Link in comments."). See "Curiosity Architecture" in voice patterns.
13. **paragraphs_punchy**: Are paragraphs brief (2-3 sentences max each)?
14. **intellectually_honest**: Does it acknowledge uncertainty, trade-offs, or complexity where appropriate?

**Passes when:** At least 12 of 14 criteria are TRUE.

### Podcast Roundup LinkedIn Teaser (Additional Rules)

When the LinkedIn post promotes a podcast roundup, these additional rules apply on top of the 14-point rubric:

1. **preserves_curiosity**: The post must NOT deliver the analysis. It teases the *shape* of insights without completing them. Think movie trailer, not executive summary. TRUE if every insight mentioned creates an open loop the reader can only close by getting the full issue.
2. **open_loop_teases**: Include 2-3 specific teases that name what was found but withhold the mechanism or explanation. Each tease should follow the pattern: "[Surprising claim] — and it has nothing to do with [the obvious explanation]."
   - Good: "There's a reason exercise stops protecting the brain as you age — and it has nothing to do with fitness."
   - Bad: "Exercise triggers GPLD1 release from the liver, which replicates cognitive benefits. But the pathway depends on NAD+..."
3. **names_sources**: Reference specific podcast hosts and guests to establish credibility, but do NOT summarize their episodes.
4. **scope_signal**: Give the reader a sense of scale ("7 findings, 5 quick hits, one editorial thesis") so they know what they're getting.
5. **under_280_words**: Roundup teasers should be tight — 200-280 words max.

## Instagram Script Quality Rubric

For spoken-word video scripts (30-90 seconds).

### Hook (3 points)
1. **stops_scroll**: First 3 seconds grab attention with surprising statement or question
2. **under_10_words**: Hook is 10 words or fewer
3. **creates_open_loop**: Hook creates curiosity that body resolves

### Body (4 points)
4. **spoken_naturally**: Written for voice, not text (contractions, conversational)
5. **single_focus**: One clear idea, no tangents
6. **evidence_included**: At least one specific fact, study, or example
7. **correct_duration**: 30-90 seconds when read at natural pace (~150 words/min)

### Close (3 points)
8. **clear_takeaway**: Viewer knows the one thing to remember
9. **has_cta**: Ends with clear next step (follow, comment, save)
10. **no_abrupt_end**: Feels complete, not cut off

**Passes when:** At least 8 of 10 criteria are TRUE.

## Regulatory Brief Quality Rubric (12-Point)

For FDA committee briefs, safety summaries, and technical regulatory documents.

### Structure (4 points)
1. **header_complete**: Header includes document type, subject identifier, and key metadata (MW, sequence, CAS# for peptides)
2. **three_section_structure**: Document has Safety Data, Regulatory Precedent, and Conclusion sections
3. **print_optimized**: Layout fits on one page (8.5"×11") with 0.75" margins
4. **table_format_used**: Route-specific data or comparisons use tables, not prose

### Safety Data Section (4 points)
5. **clinical_trials_specific**: Each trial includes n, study design, and key findings (not vague summaries)
6. **adverse_events_categorized**: AEs categorized as Common/Uncommon/Rare with specific symptoms
7. **safety_signals_explicit**: Safety signals explicitly categorized as KNOWN, THEORETICAL, or ABSENT
8. **route_considerations**: Different administration routes addressed (IV, SC, oral, etc.)

### Sources & Citations (2 points)
9. **all_sources_reputable**: Zero citations to ecommerce sites, vendors, or non-peer-reviewed sources
10. **citations_accurate**: All author names, years, and journals verified against primary source

### Conclusion (2 points)
11. **evidence_gaps_explicit**: Conclusion explicitly states what evidence is missing or limited
12. **recommendation_conditional**: Recommendation includes conditions or alternatives, not binary approve/reject

**Passes when:** All 12 criteria are TRUE. For regulatory content, there is no partial pass.

### Reasoning Consistency Framework

When creating multiple related regulatory briefs, ensure conclusions are proportional to evidence:

| Evidence Type | Relative Weight | Example |
|---------------|-----------------|---------|
| FDA-approved drugs in same class | High | Setmelanotide for melanocortin peptides |
| Human clinical trial data (direct) | High | n=107 withdrawal study |
| Non-U.S. regulatory approval | Medium | Russian Deltaran registration |
| Preclinical animal data only | Medium-Low | Mouse colitis models |
| Mechanism well-characterized | Medium | PepT1/NF-κB pathway |
| Endogenous origin | Low (safety inference only) | Natural peptide fragment |

**Consistency check:** If Document A has human trial data and Document B only has preclinical + class precedent, both can reach favorable conclusions BUT the reasoning must be explicit:
- Document A: "Direct human evidence supports safety"
- Document B: "Class-level precedent and preclinical data support safety, though direct human evidence is absent"

## Weekly Roundup Quality Rubric (12-Point)

For "This Week in Longevity" bilingual weekly newsletter. Applied to English version; Japanese version is checked separately for translation fidelity.

### Coverage (4 points)
1. **four_beats_covered**: All 4 beats (Science, Medical, Business, Tech/AI) have content? TRUE if every beat has at least one story.
2. **min_stories_per_beat**: Each beat has 2-3 stories? TRUE if no beat has fewer than 2 stories. Maximum 3 per beat to maintain focus.
3. **editors_pick_has_analysis**: Editor's Pick includes extended 150-200 word analysis? TRUE if the analysis goes beyond summary to offer unique perspective, practitioner implications, or contrarian insight.
4. **recency_verified**: All stories are from the target week's date range? TRUE if no story is older than 10 days from the newsletter date. Breaking news from just outside the window is acceptable if explicitly noted.

### Analysis Quality (4 points)
5. **analysis_not_summary**: Each story's analysis explains *why it matters*, not just *what happened*? TRUE if every story has at least one sentence addressing implications, practitioner relevance, or what to watch for. The test: could this analysis only be written by someone who understands longevity medicine, or could any news aggregator produce it?
6. **unique_perspective**: At least 3 stories include original analysis that goes beyond restating the news? TRUE if the author's expertise adds value readers can't get from the press release alone. Examples: connecting a funding round to a clinical trend, noting a study's limitations the press coverage missed, explaining what an FDA action means for clinical practice.
7. **no_link_dump**: No story is reduced to just a headline + link? TRUE if every story has both a summary sentence AND an analysis. A weekly roundup without analysis is just an RSS feed.
8. **concrete_specifics**: Stories include specific numbers, names, dates, and details? TRUE if the majority of stories cite at least one specific data point (dollar amounts, patient counts, dates, percentages).

### Structure (2 points)
9. **quick_hits_present**: Quick Hits section exists with 3-5 brief mentions? TRUE if there are items that didn't warrant full treatment but are worth flagging.
10. **what_to_watch_present**: What to Watch section exists with 1-2 forward-looking items? TRUE if the newsletter ends by pointing readers toward upcoming events, expected results, or emerging trends.

### Voice & Engagement (2 points)
11. **sources_cited**: All stories cite their primary source (journal, publication, company)? TRUE if readers can trace each claim back to an original source.
12. **forward_looking_close**: The newsletter closes by looking ahead, not summarizing what was covered? TRUE if the final section points forward rather than recapping.

**Passes when:** At least 10 of 12 criteria are TRUE, AND criteria 1, 5, and 6 are mandatory (must be TRUE).

### Japanese Version Quality Check (supplementary, not scored)

After the English version passes the 12-point rubric, check the Japanese translation for:

| Criterion | Check |
|-----------|-------|
| **Natural flow** | Does it read like a native Japanese newsletter? Not stilted machine-translation patterns. |
| **Facts preserved** | All factual claims from English version are accurately represented. |
| **Keigo level** | Semi-formal です/ます form. Authoritative but accessible (similar to Nikkei health coverage). |
| **Proper nouns** | Company names, drug names, gene names stay in original English/Latin. |
| **Domain terminology** | Standard Japanese medical terms used (再生医療, セノリティクス, エピジェネティクス, etc.) |
| **Cultural adaptation** | US-specific context (FDA, etc.) gets brief explanation where needed for Japanese readers. |
| **No raw English** | No untranslated English sentences in body text (proper nouns excepted). |

## Podcast Roundup Quality Rubric (10-Point)

For biweekly podcast roundup newsletters enriched with KB insights.

### Findings Quality (4 points)
1. **findings_are_specific**: Each finding leads with a specific, surprising claim — not a topic summary. TRUE if every finding headline could only describe ONE episode.
2. **findings_are_concise**: Each finding is 2-3 paragraphs max, not mini-essays. TRUE if no finding exceeds 150 words.
3. **deep_analysis_present**: At least 3 findings include an NGM DEEP ANALYSIS callout that surfaces **specific cross-domain mechanistic connections** from the KB. TRUE only if EVERY callout meets ALL of these:
   - **Names specific pathways, molecules, or biomarkers** (e.g., "NAD+→SIRT1→PGC-1α", "DPP-4", "Piezo1/2") — never vague references to "signaling" or "pathways"
   - **Links two or more domains that the source material does not explicitly connect** (e.g., linking senescent cell DPP-4 upregulation to GLP-1 degradation and sugar craving circuits)
   - **Adds mechanistic depth beyond what the finding already states** — not a restatement, clinical translation, or generic practitioner advice
   - FAILS if any callout reads like "For practitioners: treat X as Y" or "Consider monitoring Z" without naming the specific molecular chain that justifies the recommendation
4. **no_forced_themes**: Findings are organized by individual insight, NOT grouped into artificial thematic buckets. TRUE if there are no "Theme 1, Theme 2, Theme 3" headers.

### Writing Quality (3 points)
5. **not_recap_voice**: The writing argues and analyzes, not recaps. TRUE if no finding reads like "In this episode, [podcaster] discussed..."
6. **concrete_specifics**: Every finding includes specific names, numbers, or citations. TRUE if the Ctrl+F test for "many," "several," "significant" returns zero vague hits.
7. **tight_prose**: No paragraph exceeds 3 sentences. No finding uses filler transitions. TRUE if you could not delete a sentence without losing information.

### Structure (3 points)
8. **bottom_line_present**: Executive summary exists and captures the roundup's editorial thesis in 2-3 sentences.
9. **quick_hits_present**: 3-5 one-liner mentions for episodes that don't merit full findings.
10. **what_to_listen_to**: Top 3 episode recommendations with specific reasons.

**Passes when:** At least 8 of 10 criteria are TRUE.

## Lead Magnet Quality Rubric

For long-form educational content with diagrams.

### Content (4 points)
1. **andrew_huberman_voice**: Authoritative, educational, evidence-based tone
2. **mechanism_focus**: Explains the "why" behind claims, not just "what"
3. **appropriate_length**: ~1000 words, not padded or truncated
4. **practical_takeaways**: Includes actionable insights, not just theory

### Structure (3 points)
5. **clear_sections**: Logical flow with distinct sections
6. **mechanism_table**: Includes mechanism vs. clinical takeaway table
7. **references_section**: Properly formatted references with citations

### Diagrams (3 points)
8. **diagram_count**: 3-7 diagrams (configurable minimum)
9. **diagram_diversity**: Mix of types (pathway, comparison, process, concept)
10. **diagram_relevance**: Each diagram illustrates a specific concept from the text

**Passes when:** All 10 criteria are TRUE.

## Diagram Validation Criteria

For SVG diagrams embedded in content.

### Technical Validity (4 points)
1. **valid_viewbox**: viewBox is properly defined (e.g., "0 0 600 450")
2. **overflow_visible**: `overflow="visible"` set to prevent clipping
3. **background_set**: Background rect with #FAFAF8 fill
4. **explicit_strokes**: All shapes have `stroke="none"` unless border intentional

### Text Containment (3 points)
5. **text_within_bounds**: All text positioned at least 40px from viewBox edges
6. **container_sizing**: Containers large enough for their text (text height + 80px padding)
7. **no_overflow**: No text extends beyond its container boundaries

### Visual Quality (3 points)
8. **color_palette**: Uses only NGM palette colors (#0A0B0C, #C5A572, #E5E3DE, etc.)
9. **contrast_adequate**: Text has 4.5:1 minimum contrast ratio
10. **gold_accent_limited**: Gold (#C5A572) is ≤10% of visual area

**Passes when:** All 10 criteria are TRUE. If any fail, regenerate the diagram with specific fixes.

## How to Use These Rubrics

When self-critiquing:
1. Read through your content
2. Evaluate each criterion honestly
3. For failures, note the specific issue and location
4. Revise to address failures
5. Re-evaluate until all criteria pass

The goal is not perfection on first draft—it's iteration until quality threshold is met.
